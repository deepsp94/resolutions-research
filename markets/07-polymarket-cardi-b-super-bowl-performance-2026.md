# Polymarket: Cardi B Super Bowl halftime performance

## Bottom line

**Finding: This was a real semantic resolution controversy, but the public record does not prove that Polymarket paid the wrong side. Confidence: medium-high.**

Polymarket asked whether Cardi B would perform live and in person during the Super Bowl LX halftime show. Cardi B appeared during Bad Bunny's halftime show, danced, and mouthed words, but reporting did not establish a clear singing role.[^ap][^business-insider]

Polymarket resolved the Cardi B outcome **Yes**. The market page now records Cardi B as Yes with about **$5.6 million** volume on that outcome and about **$10.5 million** across the market.[^polymarket]

The allegation is that Cardi B made a cameo rather than a performance. The strongest defense is that Polymarket's rule was much simpler than Kalshi's: it required that she "perform live and in person," with footage of the halftime show as the primary source. It did not expressly require audible singing, a microphone, a full verse, a chorus, or instrumental performance.

The best classification is therefore **ambiguous category boundary**, not a proven platform misgrade.

## The rule

Business Insider reported Polymarket's operative test as whether Cardi B "performs live and in person" during the show.[^business-insider]

The Polymarket market page records materially similar language for outcomes in the same event: an outcome resolves Yes if the named performer performs live and in person during the Super Bowl LX halftime show; otherwise it resolves No. The primary resolution source is footage of the halftime show, though a consensus of credible reporting can also be used.[^polymarket]

That rule did not define "performs." It did not say whether dancing, lip-syncing, mouthing lyrics, appearing as part of staged choreography, or making a celebrity cameo counted.

## What happened

Cardi B joined Bad Bunny's halftime show on a staged front-porch set with other guest celebrities and artists.[^ap] Reports described her as dancing and mouthing words, while noting uncertainty about whether she was singing.[^ap][^business-insider]

Polymarket decided this counted and resolved the Cardi B outcome Yes. Business Insider reported soon after the event that Polymarket had decided Yes, although the decision had been disputed and was awaiting final review at that point.[^business-insider] AP later reported that Polymarket's contract had been resolved as Cardi B having performed, while noting that Yes had been disputed.[^ap]

The current Polymarket page records Cardi B as Yes.[^polymarket]

## The allegation against Polymarket

### 1. A cameo may not be a performance

The strongest No-side argument is ordinary-language based. Business Insider described the appearance as more like a cameo than a full performance, and the available reporting did not establish that Cardi B had a singing role.[^business-insider]

If "perform" means actively delivering music, vocals, a verse, or a defined segment, then a silent or inaudible stage appearance with dancing and mouthing words is not enough.

### 2. Polymarket gave traders little ex ante boundary guidance

Unlike Kalshi's detailed `PERFORM` rule, Polymarket's rule did not specify whether dancing, mouthing lyrics, or appearing without a microphone would count.[^business-insider][^polymarket]

That omission matters because halftime shows often include celebrity cameos, backing dancers, pre-recorded vocals, lip-syncing, and staged non-musical appearances. The rule left the decisive boundary to post-event judgment.

### 3. The different Kalshi outcome shows the category problem was real

Kalshi did not treat the same event as a clean Yes. It used an ambiguity procedure and settled Cardi B at 26 cents for Yes and 74 cents for No, citing uncertainty over whether her appearance satisfied its performance criteria.[^kalshi-market][^ap]

That does not prove Polymarket was wrong, because the platforms had different rules. It does show that the underlying category was genuinely unstable.

## Polymarket's defense

Polymarket does not appear to have published a detailed public defense explaining why Cardi B's appearance satisfied the rule. The strongest defense is implicit in the rule and final market page:

- Cardi B appeared live and in person during the official halftime show.
- She was part of the staged presentation, not merely an audience member.
- She visibly danced and mouthed words as part of the musical set.
- The Polymarket rule did not require audible singing, a microphone, a full verse, or official billing as a performer.
- The market page records the final result as Yes.[^polymarket]

Under that broader understanding, "performance" can include visible participation in a live staged halftime number even if the contribution is mostly dance, lip-sync, or cameo-style staging.

## Assessment

### Did Cardi B appear live and in person?

**Yes.**

The cited reporting and footage descriptions agree that she appeared during the halftime show.[^ap][^business-insider]

### Did she clearly perform in the narrower musical sense?

**Not established.**

The reporting specifically says it was unclear whether she was singing and describes no clear vocal or instrumental role.[^ap][^business-insider]

### Did Polymarket's written rule require that narrower musical sense?

**No, not explicitly.**

The rule used the broad phrase "performs live and in person" and named halftime-show footage as the primary source. It did not add Kalshi-style exclusions for dancing, mouthing words, or appearing without singing.[^business-insider][^polymarket]

### Was Polymarket's Yes resolution clearly wrong?

**No.**

No is a plausible ordinary-language outcome if "performance" means active musical contribution. But Yes is also plausible under Polymarket's broader wording because Cardi B visibly participated in the halftime presentation.

### Was this a real failure for the tracker?

**Yes, but as an ambiguity failure.**

Polymarket should have defined what kind of participation counts as a halftime performance before the event. The market attracted millions in volume and turned on a predictable category boundary: performance versus cameo. That is enough to include it, but not enough to say the final Yes payout was objectively false.

### Overall classification

- **Operational settlement error:** Not established
- **Semantic ambiguity:** Yes
- **Rule-design weakness:** Yes
- **Final outcome plainly wrong:** Not established
- **Best public verdict:** Defensible but under-specified Yes resolution

## Precise blocker to a stronger finding

The blocker is the lack of a crisp contractual definition and a lack of definitive public evidence about Cardi B's exact audio contribution. If Polymarket had required audible singing, No would be much stronger. If official production credits or audio evidence showed she performed a live vocal or choreographed musical segment, Yes would be much stronger. The present public record supports ambiguity.
