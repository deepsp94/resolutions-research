# Kalshi: Bernie Sanders Greensboro rally mention market

## Bottom line

**Finding: No failure. Kalshi graded the word-mention strikes (Trump, Billionaire, Middle Class) all No, but under the contract that actually governed, a word resolves Yes only if a named Source Agency documented it — not merely if the speaker said it. The outlets that confirm Sanders said the words are not named sources, and no evidence shows a named Source Agency documented the disputed words and was overridden, so a misresolution is not substantiated. Confidence: medium-high.**

The market asked what Sanders would say at his February 12, 2026 Greensboro rally. The Trump, Billionaire, and Middle Class strikes traded ~80–100% Yes and all settled No.[^kalshi] Public-radio reporters present confirm Sanders named Trump and Musk, so the words were almost certainly said.[^wfdd][^wunc] On its face that looks like a misresolution — but the contract's source standard is what controls.

An earlier draft of this case treated it as a clear failure because a "press-closed event resolves to last fair price, not No" — but **that clause is from a different Kalshi product (PRESMENTION), not the contract that governed this market.** The governing `MENTION` contract resolves a word strike Yes only if the word was "stated by `<person>` during `<address>`" *as documented by a named Source Agency*, with Kalshi holding Rule 6.3(b) discretion when the value cannot be determined.[^terms-gov] The named Source Agencies are the speaker himself plus sixteen national outlets — and WUNC/WFDD, which confirm the words, are not among them.[^terms-gov] So the intuitive complaint ("he obviously said it, therefore Yes") misreads the rule. For a regional theater rally that those national outlets likely did not live-broadcast or transcribe, the value was plausibly undeterminable from the source hierarchy, and resolving No is a defensible application of the contract — not a proven error.

- **Event:** `KXBERNIEMENTION-26FEB13` — "What will Bernie say at his Greensboro rally?"[^kalshi]
- **Underlying:** Bernie Sanders, Carolina Theatre, Greensboro NC, February 12, 2026 (Fighting Oligarchy / "Common Thread" tour)[^kalshi]
- **Structure:** word-mention strikes (Trump, Billionaire, Middle Class, and others); ~$3.63M volume[^kalshi][^gambling]
- **Governing contract:** `MENTION` as it existed before the event (archived January 11, 2026). Resolves a word Yes if "stated by `<person>` during `<address>`" as documented by the Source Agency; Source Agency = `<person>` plus NYT, AP, Bloomberg, Reuters, Axios, Politico, Semafor, The Information, WaPo, WSJ, ABC, CBS, CNN, Fox News, MSNBC, NBC; video used primarily; if the value "cannot be determined," Kalshi may resolve under Rule 6.3(b).[^terms-gov]
- **Settlement:** Trump, Billionaire, and Middle Class strikes all resolved No (marked "Determined"), after trading ~80–100% Yes.[^kalshi]

## What happened

Sanders spoke at the Carolina Theatre in Greensboro on February 12, 2026 as part of his Fighting Oligarchy tour.[^wunc][^journalnow] The Kalshi event listed word-mention strikes that resolve Yes if Sanders said the word; common strikes (Trump, Billionaire, Middle Class) priced at 80–100%, expecting Yes.[^kalshi] Public-radio wire reporting from WFDD and WUNC — with a reporter present — quotes Sanders referencing "President Donald Trump" and naming Elon Musk, Mark Zuckerberg, and Jeff Bezos.[^wfdd][^wunc] An ABC affiliate carried video of the appearance.[^abc]

Despite this, Kalshi settled the Trump, Billionaire, and Middle Class strikes all No, with no public explanation.[^kalshi][^gambling][^quasa]

## The allegation against Kalshi

That Kalshi misresolved a ~$3.63M mention market: strikes that traded near-certain Yes settled No even though Sanders demonstrably said qualifying words.[^gambling][^quasa] The allegation assumes that because the words were said, the strikes had to resolve Yes.

## Kalshi's defense

The defense is the contract's source standard. The Underlying is the words Sanders used *as documented by a Source Agency*, with video used primarily and the named outlets' transcripts as backup; if that hierarchy yields no determination, Kalshi resolves under Rule 6.3(b).[^terms-gov] The rally was a regional event. If none of the sixteen named national outlets published a qualifying live broadcast or transcript, and no usable live stream from the speaker was treated as available, then the Expiration Value could not be established from the source hierarchy, and the No grades are a discretionary determination rather than a contract violation. The WUNC/WFDD reports that confirm the words are not from named Source Agencies and therefore do not, by themselves, force a Yes.

## Assessment

### Were the words actually said?

**Almost certainly yes.** WFDD and WUNC, with a reporter present, quote Sanders naming Trump and Musk; those are core terms of the Fighting Oligarchy tour.[^wfdd][^wunc]

### Did the earlier "last fair price" basis for a failure hold up?

**No.** That clause is from PRESMENTION, a different product. The governing `MENTION` contract (archived January 11, 2026) contains no press-closed/last-fair-price rule and no "partial snippets resolve Yes" rule; resolution runs on Source-Agency documentation with Rule 6.3(b) as the fallback.[^terms-gov]

### Does "the words were said" establish a Yes under the contract?

**No.** The contract requires the word to be documented by a named Source Agency, not merely uttered. WUNC/WFDD — the outlets confirming the words — are not named sources, so their reporting does not by itself satisfy the Payout Criterion.[^terms-gov]

### Is a misresolution established?

**No.** No source shows that a named Source Agency (the speaker's own live stream, or a listed outlet's live broadcast/transcript) documented the disputed words and that Kalshi nonetheless graded No. For a regional rally the national named outlets plausibly did not cover in qualifying form, resolving No via the source hierarchy and Rule 6.3(b) is a defensible reading. The burden of proving a failure is not met.

### Is there any fair criticism of Kalshi?

**Yes, but it is design, not misresolution.** Strikes for words Sanders clearly said settled No after trading ~90%, Kalshi published no rationale, and it recertified a rewritten mention contract two weeks later that added explicit last-fair-price handling for exactly this situation.[^gambling][^recert] That sequence suggests the original framework handled regional, lightly-covered events poorly — but applying the rule as written, with no last-fair-price mechanism then in place, is not a resolution failure. This mirrors the Oscars (#5) and Miami high-temperature (#22) cases, where Kalshi followed a written rule that later proved weak.

### Overall classification

- **Words said:** Almost certainly yes
- **"Last fair price" remedy:** Did not exist in the governing contract
- **Source-Agency documentation of the words, then overridden:** Not shown
- **Final misresolution:** Not established
- **Weak contract design (since rewritten):** Yes
- **Best public verdict:** No failure

## Precise blocker to a stronger finding

None material to the verdict. The decisive contract facts are verified from the archived governing `MENTION` terms and the settled market page, and the failure is unsubstantiated on the record. One residual would *strengthen* the case in either direction but is not needed to reach No failure: confirmation of whether a named Source Agency (the speaker's own live stream, or a listed outlet's live broadcast/transcript) captured the disputed words. If such a record clearly existed and was overridden, the case would become a Failure; absent it, the No grades stand as a defensible rule application.
