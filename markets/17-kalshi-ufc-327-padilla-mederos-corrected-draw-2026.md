# Kalshi: UFC 327 Padilla vs. Mederos corrected draw

## Bottom line

**Finding: This was a real rule-design controversy, but Kalshi appears to have followed its written rule. Confidence: high.**

Kalshi listed a market on the UFC 327 fight between Christopher Padilla and MarQuel Mederos. Padilla was initially announced as the winner, so Kalshi paid Padilla contracts. The scorecards were later corrected and the fight became a majority draw.[^predictionnews][^mmanews]

Kalshi's market page stated that a draw or no contest would resolve 50/50, but it also stated that revisions after expiration would not be considered. The page warned that if the official result was reversed or changed after the contract expired, that change would not affect market resolution.[^kalshi]

So the criticism is real but narrow: Kalshi's settlement no longer matched the corrected sports result. The defense is stronger: the written rule explicitly chose the initial/expiration-time result over later revisions.

## Market details

- **Venue:** Kalshi
- **Event:** `KXUFCFIGHT-26APR11PADMED`
- **Market shown:** `KXUFCFIGHT-26APR11PADMED-PAD`
- **Question:** whether Christopher Padilla would win the Padilla vs. Mederos professional MMA fight originally scheduled for April 11, 2026.
- **Rule for draw/no contest:** resolves 50/50.
- **Revision rule:** revisions after expiration are not considered.
- **Volume shown on Kalshi page:** about $1.1 million.[^kalshi]

## What happened

MMA News reported that Padilla was originally announced as the winner by majority decision. Later in the broadcast, the score was corrected and the fight was ruled a majority draw.[^mmanews]

PredictionNews reported that Kalshi resolved its market based on the initial Padilla victory, paying Padilla contracts and paying nothing on Mederos contracts. It also reported that Kalshi's rules stated the initial decision would control and that revisions after expiration would not be considered.[^predictionnews]

The Kalshi market page contains the same key language: draws resolve 50/50, but revisions after expiration do not count, including reversals or changes to the official result after the contract expires.[^kalshi]

## The allegation against Kalshi

### 1. The final sports result was a draw

Anyone looking up the fight after the correction would see that the fight was not a Padilla win. Kalshi's payout therefore did not match the corrected bout result.

### 2. The draw clause made the outcome feel especially wrong

The market itself said draws resolve 50/50.[^kalshi] After the correction, the fight was a draw.[^mmanews] Traders could reasonably object that the draw clause was the natural rule to apply.

### 3. Initial-result rules can mislead users about what a sports market represents

PredictionNews made the broader point: resolving on an initial outcome creates a clean administrative cutoff but can mislead someone who expects a market settlement to reflect the final, corrected event result.[^predictionnews]

## Kalshi's defense

Kalshi's defense is explicit in the rules.

The market warned that revisions after expiration would not be considered and that later reversals or changes to the official result would not affect resolution.[^kalshi] PredictionNews also described the case as analogous to Kalshi's Oscars viewership market, where Kalshi used an initial figure despite later revisions.[^predictionnews]

Under that rule, a later scorecard correction did not change the payout even if it changed the official sports record.

## Assessment

### Was Padilla initially announced as the winner?

**Yes.**

MMA News and PredictionNews both report that Padilla was initially declared the winner.[^mmanews][^predictionnews]

### Was the result later corrected to a draw?

**Yes.**

MMA News reports that the UFC broadcast later announced a correction to majority draw.[^mmanews]

### Did Kalshi's rule exclude later revisions?

**Yes.**

The Kalshi page expressly says revisions after expiration are not considered.[^kalshi]

### Did Kalshi clearly violate its written rule?

**No.**

The written rule appears to support Kalshi's payout based on the initial/expiration-time result.

### Was this a real failure for the tracker?

**Yes, as a rule-design and user-expectation failure.**

The market settlement diverged from the corrected sports outcome, and that divergence matters for a public tracker. But the error, if any, is in choosing a rigid initial-result rule, not in Kalshi ignoring its own stated terms.

### Overall classification

- **Operational settlement error:** Not established
- **Initial official result vs corrected result:** Yes
- **Kalshi followed written rule:** Likely yes
- **Rule-design weakness:** Yes
- **Best public verdict:** Defensible settlement under explicit rule; poor proxy for final fight result

## Precise blocker to a stronger finding

To show a Kalshi resolution error, one would need evidence that the contract had not expired before the correction or that Kalshi applied a different rule than the one shown on the market page. The reviewed sources do not show that.
